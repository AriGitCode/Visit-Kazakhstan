Mangystau Region
Mangystau is home to a geological wonder called the "Shortogay" or "Devil's Balls." These are giant, perfectly round boulders scattered across the desert. Scientists believe they formed millions of years ago from layers of sediment hardening around a core, then eroding away to reveal the smooth spheres. Locals have their own legends about the balls, attributing them to everything from dragons' eggs to petrified apples from a mythical garden.

Almaty Region
Nestled in the foothills of the Trans-Ili Alatau mountains lies Tamgaly Tas, an open-air museum of rock art. Over 2,000 petroglyphs (images carved into rock) adorn the cliffs, dating back thousands of years. These carvings depict animals like deer and wolves, scenes of hunting and rituals, and even warriors on horseback. Tamgaly Tas offers a window into the lives and beliefs of the ancient people who inhabited the region.

Turkestan
Turkestan is one of Kazakhstan's oldest cities, boasting a rich history dating back to the 4th century. It served as a vital stop along the Silk Road, a network of trade routes that connected East and West for centuries. Today, the city is a treasure trove of historical sites, including the impressive Mausoleum of Khoja Ahmed Yasawi, a UNESCO World Heritage Site known for its turquoise dome and intricate tilework.

Wildlife
The majestic snow leopard calls Kazakhstan home! With an estimated population of 141-183 individuals, Kazakhstan is one of the few countries where you can find these elusive cats roaming the Tian Shan, Altai, Zhetysu Alatau, and Saur mountain ranges. Snow leopards are sadly classified as vulnerable and included in Kazakhstan's Vulnerable Species List.
